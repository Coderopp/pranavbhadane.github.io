The Death of the Chat Interface (And the Business Models That Die With It)

It's not a technology story. It's an economics story. And the economics are about to break a lot of business models.

I want to make an argument that's bigger than UX.

The AI industry is going through a three-phase evolution — Chat, Copilot, Agent — and most commentary treats this as a technology story. Better models, better interfaces, less friction.

It's not a technology story. It's an economics story. And the economics are about to break a lot of business models.

The Three Phases Are Really About Who Does the Work

In 2023, we vibecoded. Alt-Tab to ChatGPT, type a prompt, copy the code, paste it into VS Code, run it, find the error, go back to ChatGPT, paste the error, get a fix, paste the fix. Repeat.

That felt revolutionary for about three months. Then I realized what was actually happening: the AI was the advisor, and I was still the worker. The cost of execution hadn't changed. I was still doing every click, every copy-paste, every context switch. The AI just gave me better instructions.

Phase two — copilots. GitHub Copilot in your editor. Canva's AI in your canvas. Gemini sidebar in your browser. The AI now had context. It could see your file, your design, your page. But I was still the decision-maker and the executor. I approved every suggestion. I did the actual work, just with better autocomplete.

Phase three — agents. Cursor, Claude Code, Windsurf. These tools don't suggest — they do. They write the code, run the tests, read the errors, fix the bugs, and come back with a working result. Photoshop Generative Fill doesn't suggest edits — it makes them at the pixel level.

Here's the pattern nobody's naming explicitly:

  • Phase 1: Human does 100% of execution. AI advises.
  • Phase 2: Human does 70% of execution. AI assists.
  • Phase 3: Human sets intent. AI does 95% of execution.

That's not a UX evolution. That's a labor restructuring. The human moves from worker to manager. The AI moves from advisor to employee.

And in every domain where this transition has completed — coding, design — the cost of execution has collapsed. What used to take a junior developer a full day takes Cursor fifteen minutes. What used to take a designer three hours of retouching takes Generative Fill thirty seconds.

The browser is the last major platform where the human is still the worker.

Why the Browser Is Stuck (And It's Not Just CAPTCHAs)

The standard explanation for why browser agents lag behind is technical: the open web is hostile. Anti-scraping measures, CAPTCHAs, dynamic DOM elements, login walls. All real.

But I think the deeper reason is political.

Think about what happens when a browser agent actually works. You say "book me the cheapest flight to London next Thursday." The agent opens Google Flights, compares options, navigates to the airline's site, fills in your details, selects your seat, and books the ticket.

What just happened to Expedia?

Their entire business model depends on you spending time on their website. Their revenue comes from your eyeballs seeing their sponsored listings, their upsell prompts ("Add a hotel?"), their carefully designed booking flow that nudges you toward more expensive options. Every minute you spend on Expedia is a minute they monetize.

A browser agent bypasses all of that. It goes straight to the data, gets the cheapest option, executes, and leaves. Expedia's UI becomes invisible. Their upsell engine never fires. Their engagement metrics collapse.

Scale this across every SaaS company. Salesforce's carefully designed dashboard — bypassed. Workday's onboarding flow — invisible. ServiceNow's ticket management interface — an AI navigates it in seconds instead of a human clicking through it for minutes.

Every SaaS company's business model is built on humans spending time in their interface. Browser agents threaten to make those interfaces invisible.

This is why the browser is stuck in phase two. It's not just that the web is technically hostile. It's that the companies that control the web have an economic incentive to keep it hostile to agents. CAPTCHAs aren't just anti-bot security — they're moats that protect attention-based business models.

The "Action Gap" isn't a technical gap. It's a political gap.

The Infrastructure Bet

So who wins?

When I was working on InstiGPT and ShipAI, I learned something that applies directly here: generalist models fail the moment they need specific context. A general chatbot can tell you about Salesforce. But it can't navigate your Salesforce — your login, your permissions, your custom fields, your data schema.

The winning browser agent platform needs to solve four problems simultaneously: session management (inheriting your cookies and authentication, not creating separate logins), sandboxed execution (running in an isolated browser environment so a mistake doesn't compromise your system), granular permissions (read-only for sensitive pages, human approval for irreversible actions), and enterprise trust (SOC2, audit logs, the boring infrastructure that makes CIOs say yes).

This is what companies like RISA are betting on. Not a smarter model — everyone will have smart models. The bet is on infrastructure: the plumbing that lets agents safely act on a hostile web.

It's the same pattern from cloud computing. AWS didn't win because they had the best servers. They won because they built the best infrastructure layer — the security, the permissions, the scalability, the billing — that let everyone else build on top.

The RISA bet is that browser agents need their own infrastructure layer. And the company that builds it captures the platform economics.

The Real Question

Here's where I land, and it's not about technology.

If browser agents succeed — if the chat interface truly dies and agents start executing on our behalf — then we're looking at a massive redistribution of value.

Today, value flows to the companies that control the interface: Expedia captures value because you book through their site. Salesforce captures value because you spend hours in their dashboard. Every SaaS company's pricing model assumes human time spent in their product.

Tomorrow, value flows to the companies that control the agent layer: the platforms that manage intent, context, execution, and trust. The SaaS interface becomes a commodity — just a data backend that agents access programmatically.

The death of the chat interface isn't a UX story. It's a story about who controls the last mile of execution — and the trillions of dollars that flow through it.

We spent 2023 talking to AI. We spent 2025 working alongside AI. By 2026, we'll be delegating to AI.

The question isn't whether this happens. It's who captures the value when it does.
