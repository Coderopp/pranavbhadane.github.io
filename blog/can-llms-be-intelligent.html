Can Large Language Models Be Intelligent?

Let me start with what they actually are.

Large Language Models predict the next word. That's it. Given a sequence of text, they calculate which token is statistically most likely to follow. Through sheer scale — billions of parameters, trillions of training tokens — this one trick produces something remarkable: coherent paragraphs, logical arguments, working code, medical diagnoses, poetry that can make you cry.

But here's a question that's been nagging me.

A 10-year-old can form perfectly meaningful sentences. They can even pass IQ tests. Would anyone say they've reached the peak of their intelligence? Obviously not. We intuitively understand that there's something above linguistic competence — something that a 10-year-old hasn't developed yet and that a 30-year-old physicist has.

So if forming meaningful sentences isn't intelligence, what is?

I think there are two separate limits standing between LLMs and genuine intelligence. And they're fundamentally different in nature.

The First Limit: The Body Problem

An 18-year-old can talk, drive a car, use a calculator, cook dinner, comfort a crying friend, play basketball, and plan a road trip. One brain. One body. Infinite applications.

An LLM can talk but can't drive. A self-driving car can drive but can't talk. A robot can move objects but can't diagnose your medical condition. Each capability lives in a separate system, built by a different team, running on different hardware.

This isn't a failure of intelligence. It's a failure of platform.

The human brain is the most extraordinary general-purpose computing platform ever built. It runs linguistic intelligence, spatial reasoning, motor control, emotional processing, and abstract thought on the same biological substrate. It integrates all of them seamlessly, in real-time, while also keeping your heart beating and your lungs breathing.

The AI industry knows this is a problem, and they're building workarounds. Tesla and Figure AI are building humanoid robots — giving AI a physical body. Anthropic built "computer use" so Claude can move a cursor and click buttons — a rudimentary digital body. Google DeepMind's robotics research is trying to connect language understanding to physical manipulation.

But notice what's happening: they're building a separate body for each domain. A different architecture for driving, a different one for walking, a different one for surgery. The 18-year-old does all of this with one brain. The AI industry needs an entirely new system for each one.

This is a real limitation. But I don't think it's the interesting one.

The Second Limit: The Thinking Problem

There's a category of intelligence that has nothing to do with bodies. It's not about what you think about. It's about how you think.

Consider a junior developer and a senior developer. Both write syntactically correct, working code. The code compiles in both cases. But the senior developer does something the junior developer can't: they see the system. They know that changing one module will break the payment system three layers down. They understand trade-offs that aren't visible in any single file. They think in architectures, not functions.

That's systems thinking. And it's just one of several higher-order thinking modes that sit above language:

Abstract thinking — working with concepts that don't exist concretely. When you say "justice is a blindfolded woman holding scales," you're manipulating symbols that have no physical form. LLMs can produce abstract-sounding text. But are they generating novel abstractions, or recombining ones they've seen?

Metacognition — thinking about your own thinking. "I'm stuck on this problem. Why am I stuck? Am I approaching it wrong?" This is what chain-of-thought reasoning attempts to replicate. When a model says "wait, let me reconsider," is it genuinely monitoring its own cognition, or is it producing text that describes reconsidering?

Critical thinking — evaluating information rather than just processing it. Not "what does this article say?" but "should I believe it? What's the author's bias? What evidence is missing?" LLMs tend to treat all input as equally valid. They can be trained to push back (through RLHF), but that skepticism is reactive, not proactive.

Moral reasoning — not "what can I do?" but "what should I do?" A doctor who chooses not to perform an unnecessary but profitable procedure is exercising moral reasoning. In AI, ethics are pre-installed through alignment training. The model doesn't arrive at moral conclusions through reflection. The morality is bolted on from outside.

Transfer learning — the human kind. You learn patience from fishing and apply it to studying for exams. You learn negotiation from arguing with siblings and use it in job interviews. Each conversation an LLM has starts from zero. There's no continuous life experience to draw from.

The AI industry has workarounds for these too. Chain-of-thought for reasoning. Multi-agent systems for collaboration. RLHF for judgment. Memory frameworks for continuity.

But here's what bothers me: every one of these workarounds replicates the output of higher-order thinking, not the process. They produce text that looks like reasoning. They don't actually reason. They generate responses that look like self-reflection. They don't actually reflect.

The distinction matters. And it matters most when we get to creativity.

The Creativity Ceiling

Einstein once said something to the effect that creativity is applying something completely to a new domain — seeing what everyone else has seen and thinking what no one else has thought.

He didn't invent relativity from nothing. He took the behavior of light, combined it with thought experiments about trains and clocks, borrowed mathematics from Riemann's geometry, and produced something that none of those individual domains contained. The creative act was the bridge between domains that had no obvious connection.

Darwin did the same thing. He connected pigeon breeding — artificial selection that farmers had practiced for centuries — with finch diversity in the Galápagos and produced natural selection. He saw the same pattern operating in two completely different contexts.

George de Mestral noticed burrs sticking to his dog's fur after a walk and invented Velcro. Steve Jobs saw that a phone, a music player, and an internet browser wanted to be the same device.

What do all of these have in common? They require seeing the large picture and the small picture simultaneously.

The large picture is the structural similarity between domains that don't obviously connect. The small picture is the precise mechanism that transfers — the specific element that maps from one domain to another.

Creativity lives in the simultaneous operation of both. Zoom out to see the connection. Zoom in to make it precise. Hold both focal lengths at once.

A novelist holds the theme of the entire book, the arc of this chapter, the rhythm of this sentence, and the choice of this specific word — all at once. Each level informs the others. An architect holds the city skyline, the building's purpose, the floor plan's flow, and the material's texture simultaneously.

LLMs process sequentially. Token by token. They can simulate zooming in and out if you prompt them to. But the simultaneity — holding multiple scales of meaning at once and seeing the pattern that connects them — that's something fundamentally different.

Can LLMs recombine known ideas efficiently? Absolutely. They're very fast librarians. But the librarian finds books. Einstein wrote a new one that contradicted every book in the library.

The Question We're Not Asking

Here's where I land, and I think this is the part most people in the AI conversation are missing.

The entire AI industry isn't actually pursuing intelligence. It's pursuing the cost reduction of intelligence.

Why does OpenAI build a reasoning model? Because a senior consultant costs $500/hour to analyze a contract. If an LLM can do 80% of that analysis for $0.02, that's the product. Why does Anthropic build tool calling? Because an executive assistant costs $60,000 a year. If an agent can handle scheduling for a subscription fee, that's the business.

Every capability — tool use, reasoning, memory, multi-agent collaboration — maps directly to a job function that currently costs money. The goal was never to create a mind. It was to create something just good enough to replace the expensive parts of human cognition.

And this explains a curious pattern: the types of intelligence being replicated are the ones with economic value. Linguistic intelligence? Huge market. Logical-mathematical reasoning? Enormous demand. Spatial understanding? Growing fast.

Existential intelligence? No investment. Genuine creativity? No reliable product. Moral reasoning that the model arrives at through its own reflection? No business model.

The intelligence that gets built is the intelligence that can be sold.

Gartner predicts that by 2029, 80% of customer support issues will be handled by AI agents. That's not an intelligence milestone. That's a cost reduction milestone. The question "is this intelligent?" is irrelevant to the business case. The question that matters is "is this cheap enough?"

So when someone asks me "Can LLMs be intelligent?", I think the honest answer is: the question itself reveals more than any answer could.

We're not building intelligence. We're building a cheaper substitute for intelligence and calling it the same thing.

The real question is: what happens to the genuine article — the abstract thinking, the cross-domain creativity, the moral reflection, the existential questioning — when the market decides it doesn't need to pay for it anymore?

What are we actually building? And what are we choosing to lose?
