Case Study · Creativity × Virality

The Anatomy of a Cultural Phenomenon

How Dhurandhar became a ₹1,350 crore film by borrowing everything and inventing nothing — and what that teaches us about building things that spread.

A two-part essay on recombination, distribution, and the craft of making work that travels.

There's a quote often attributed to Einstein: "The secret to creativity is knowing how to hide your sources."

It's a beautiful line. It's also not his. The saying actually traces back to a British philosopher named C.E.M. Joad, who wrote in 1926 that "the height of originality is skill in concealing origins." Over six decades, the phrase mutated — from "originality" to "creativity," from "concealing origins" to "hiding your sources" — until someone, in 1989, pinned it on Einstein. And it stuck.

Notice the irony: a quote about creativity hiding its sources had its own source completely hidden. That's not a failure of attribution. That's the mechanism of creativity itself, working exactly as described.

I've been thinking about this mechanism ever since I watched Dhurandhar — Aditya Dhar's spy thriller that became the highest-grossing Indian film of 2025 and crossed ₹1,350 crore worldwide. It's a film that, on paper, borrows almost everything. The plot structure. The songs. The character archetypes. Even the casting philosophy has precedent. And yet, it felt like nothing else in Indian cinema. It hit like an original.

This essay is an attempt to understand why. Not as a film review — there are enough of those — but as a case study in how great creative work actually gets made and, more importantly, how it spreads. Because the two questions are not separate. The anatomy of the creation and the anatomy of its virality are the same skeleton.

Part I

The Anatomy of Recombination

Every piece of creative work that feels original is, when you look closely enough, an act of recombination. The skill is not in inventing from nothing — it's in choosing what to borrow, transforming it in transit, and assembling the pieces in an arrangement nobody has seen before.

Dhurandhar is a masterclass in this. Let me break it down layer by layer — not what the film contains, but what it borrows and what it transforms.

Story Architecture: The Mole Who Climbs

Infernal Affairs / The Departed / Donnie Brasco → Recontextualized into South Asian geopolitics

The core plot — an intelligence operative infiltrating a criminal syndicate, rising through its ranks, sending intel back while losing himself in the role — belongs to one of cinema's oldest archetypes. Hong Kong's Infernal Affairs did it. Scorsese remade it. Donnie Brasco explored the psychological cost.

What Dhar did was transplant this skeleton into a context where it had never been placed: the real Lyari gang wars of Karachi, the IC-814 hijacking, the 2001 Parliament attack. The borrowed structure became a vehicle for a story that felt specific, grounded, and unprecedented. The architecture was old. The geography was new. And that combination is what made it feel original.

Music: Nostalgia as an Emotional Weapon

Hasan Jahangir / R.D. Burman / Bappi Lahiri / Flipperachi → Remixed into narrative punctuation

Shashwat Sachdev's soundtrack is a study in what I'd call strategic borrowing. Rather than compose entirely original music, the film deliberately reached for songs audiences already carried emotional associations with — and then recontextualized them.

Hasan Jahangir's 1987 "Hawa Hawa" — a Pakistani pop song — was used to introduce Sanjay Dutt's corrupt Pakistani police officer. The makers paid $50,000 for the rights. Asha Bhosle and R.D. Burman's "Dum Maro Dum" appeared. Bappi Lahiri's "Ramba Ho Ho Ho" was remixed. The Punjabi folk classic "Na Dil De Pardesi Nu" was reworked into the title track with Hanumankind and Jasmine Sandlas.

And then there's FA9LA — an Arabic track by Bahraini rapper Flipperachi, used for Akshaye Khanna's entry. This wasn't a remix of nostalgia. It was an injection of the completely unfamiliar — a song most Indian audiences had never heard — that became the most viral moment in the film.

The result: the first Bollywood soundtrack where every track charted simultaneously on the Spotify Global Top 200.

The Pattern

Mix the deeply familiar with the genuinely alien. The nostalgia lowers the audience's defences. The unfamiliar creates the shareable moment.

Casting: Hierarchy-Breaking as a Creative Act

Bollywood star system hierarchy → Inverted through performance-first casting

Aditya Dhar's brief to casting director Mukesh Chhabra was reportedly simple: "Bring me great actors, new or old, big or small, it doesn't matter." The result was a cast that broke every rule of Bollywood's star hierarchy.

Television actors Rakesh Bedi and Gaurav Gera — known for comedy — played menacing political operators. R. Madhavan, a Tamil cinema star, played the intelligence chief. Sara Arjun, a South Indian child-actress-turned-adult-debutante, played the female lead. Ranveer Singh, the biggest "star" in the cast, played the least flashy role — a spy whose job is to be invisible.

This is recombination applied to people. You take actors the audience has one mental model for, place them in roles that contradict that model, and the dissonance itself becomes the performance. The audience can't stop watching because their expectations keep getting broken.

Cinematic Language: Violence as Texture, Chapters as Rhythm

Tarantino's chapter structure / Sicario's mood → Applied to a 214-minute Indian espionage saga

The film is structured in eight chapters — Stranger in the Land of Shadows, The Bastard King of Lyari, Bullets and Roses, and so on. Every Pakistani character gets a Tarantino-style introduction with slow-motion entry shots and name plates. The hero gets none. He enters in the background, anonymous — which is exactly how a spy should enter.

The visual palette borrows from Sicario's tension and Zero Dark Thirty's procedural realism, but applies them to Karachi's Lyari neighbourhood — a setting Western espionage cinema has never touched. Again: old grammar, new location, original feeling.

When you stack these layers, a pattern emerges. In every creative dimension of the film — plot, music, casting, visual language — the move is the same: borrow a proven structure, transplant it into an unfamiliar context, and let the friction between the familiar structure and the unfamiliar context create the feeling of originality.

This is not plagiarism. This is not even homage. This is the actual mechanism by which original work gets made. And understanding this mechanism is the first step to understanding why the work spread the way it did.

Part II

The Virality Stack

Here's the question that matters: Pathaan had Shah Rukh Khan's comeback. War 2 had Hrithik. Singham Again had the entire cop universe. These were all spy or action films with bigger pre-existing fanbases. Yet Dhurandhar, an A-certified, 3.5-hour film with no franchise history, outperformed most of them.

Why?

Because virality isn't one thing. It's a stack — multiple layers of shareability that compound on each other. Each layer creates a different type of conversation, and the conversations feed each other. Here's how Dhurandhar built its stack:

1

Nostalgia as Distribution

Every remixed classic song — "Hawa Hawa," "Dum Maro Dum," "Ramba Ho" — came with a pre-existing audience. When someone hears a song they already love reimagined in a new context, their instinct is to share the comparison. "Have you heard what they did to Hawa Hawa?" is a more powerful distribution trigger than any poster or trailer. The music didn't just serve the film — it recruited audiences who weren't even looking for a spy thriller.

2

The Alien Moment: FA9LA as a Meme Engine

Akshaye Khanna walking in to an Arabic track nobody in India had heard before became the single most clipped, shared, and meme-d moment of the film. FA9LA topped Apple Music's Top 100: India and Spotify's Viral 50. It worked precisely because it was unfamiliar — people shared it as a discovery, not a reference. The nostalgia songs brought in the existing audience. The alien moment created the new one.

3

Casting Choices That Generate Debate

Putting Rakesh Bedi — a comedian audiences associate with 90s slapstick — in a serious political role generated its own news cycle. Every "unexpected casting" became a talking point. People who hadn't seen the film were discussing it because the casting decisions themselves were content. The conversation wasn't "should I watch Dhurandhar?" It was "can you believe Rakesh Bedi in Dhurandhar?" — and that conversation pulled people into theatres.

4

The Two-Part Structure as an Anticipation Engine

The decision to split the film into two parts — made during post-production when Dhar realized he had seven hours of footage — turned the ending of Part 1 into a cliffhanger that sustained conversation for months. The sequel, Dhurandhar: The Revenge, arrived in March 2026 and opened with over ₹100 crore on Day 1. The first film's virality didn't decay between parts — it compounded. Each part drove viewership for the other.

5

Platform Adoption: When the Product Becomes the Tool

Instagram added a custom Dhurandhar-inspired font to its Edits app, letting users create bold captions that looked like the movie poster. This is the rarest and most powerful layer of the virality stack — when a platform absorbs your creation into its own toolkit. The film stopped being content to be consumed and became a tool to create with. Over 70 national brands and nearly 400 advertisers aligned with the sequel's release, spanning everything from FMCG to automobiles.

The Compounding Effect

No single layer of this stack was new. Nostalgia, memes, casting debates, cliffhangers, brand tie-ins — all old tactics. What made Dhurandhar different is that all five layers fired simultaneously, and each layer amplified the others. That's not marketing. That's architecture.

Look at the box office data and you see the compounding in action. Day 1 collected ₹24 crore. Day 3 jumped to ₹35.5 crore — a pattern almost never seen, where the film grew instead of declining. Week 2 collected ₹253 crore, actually higher than Week 1's ₹207 crore. This is what word-of-mouth compounding looks like in numbers: the film's sustained second-week performance was driven by organic audience demand, not marketing spend.

Even on Netflix, the pattern held. The film debuted at 7.6 million views in Week 1, then peaked at 8.2 million in Week 2 — the first Indian film to grow on streaming in its second week. It maintained a Top 10 presence for ten consecutive weeks.

When the sequel's announcement landed, viewership jumped again — from 1 million in Week 7 to 2.3 million in Week 8. The franchise created its own feedback loop.

What This Teaches Anyone Building Anything

Dhurandhar is a film. But the principles underneath it apply to any act of creation — a product, a company, a campaign, a piece of writing. Here's what I take from it:

Lesson 01

Originality is not invention. It's the skill of placing borrowed elements in contexts where they've never appeared. Stop trying to create from zero. Start asking: what existing idea would be transformative if I moved it into a completely different domain?

Lesson 02

The familiar is your distribution channel. Nostalgia, recognizable patterns, known faces in unknown roles — these are not creative compromises. They're the handles your audience grabs to pull themselves into your work. The unfamiliar is what makes them stay. But they need the familiar to walk through the door.

Lesson 03

Virality is not a feature you add — it's a stack you build. One shareable element is a moment. Two is a coincidence. Five compounding layers is a cultural phenomenon. Design every layer of your work to generate a different type of conversation.

Lesson 04

The most powerful distribution happens when your creation becomes someone else's tool. Instagram didn't promote Dhurandhar — they absorbed its aesthetic into their product. Ask yourself: can someone use my work to express themselves? If yes, it will travel further than any campaign.

Lesson 05

Hierarchy is the enemy of resonance. Dhar's casting brief was four words: "bring me great actors." Not big actors. Not safe actors. Great ones. The best leaders and creators share this instinct — they optimize for the quality of each component, not its pedigree. A comedian in a menacing role creates more energy than a star in a comfortable one.

The quote about hiding your sources was never Einstein's. The plot structure was borrowed from Hong Kong and Hollywood. The songs belonged to other decades. The casting broke every convention. And the result was the most original-feeling Indian film of 2025. That's the whole lesson, really. The secret to originality is skilled recombination. And the secret to virality is building enough layers that people can't stop talking about the recombination.
